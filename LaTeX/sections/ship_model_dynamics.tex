\section{Drone Ship Physics Model }

Very important part of my project is to have a good physics model of the drone ship. 
That model will be used to simulate IMU and GPS sensor readings, as well as to provide a realistic environment for testing control algorithms.

First step is to model the buoyancy of the ship. The ship will be modeled as a rigid body with a certain mass, width, length, and draft. Currently model does not assume any particular shape for the ship, but it will be important to consider the shape in future iterations to improve the accuracy of the model. The buoyancy force will be calculated based on the 4 points of the ship: the bow, stern, port, and starboard. The buoyancy force will be calculated as the weight of the displaced water, which is equal to the volume of the submerged part of the ship multiplied by the density of water and gravity. 

Same points will be used to calculate the wave drag force. In each of 4 points there is a wave drag force acting in the normal direction to the surface of the water. Forces are scaled to the size of the ship. 

This approach captures the essential physics of the problem while keeping the model computationally efficient. Placement of these points is visible in the figure below
\ref{fig:ship_buoyancy_points}.


That model will be used to simulate the motion of the ship in response to control inputs and environmental forces.

\subsection{Introduction}

The physics engine utilizes a multi-point "floater" system to calculate the upward force of displacement, wave drag force, coupled with linear damping and keel-drag curves to simulate water resistance.







\subsubsection{Model of Buoyancy}
According to Archimedes' Principle, the buoyancy force $F_b$ is equal to the weight of the displaced fluid. In this implementation, we approximate the submerged volume by checking the depth $h$ of $N$ discrete points (floaters) relative to the water surface $y_{sea}$.For each floater $i$, the submergence factor $s_i$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
s_i = \text{clamp01}\left(\frac{y_{sea} - y_i}{h_{max}}\right)
\label{equation:submergence_equation}
\end{equation}

The total buoyancy force $\mathbf{F}_B$ applied at the floater position is:


\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_B = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (k_b \cdot s_i) \hat{\mathbf{\hat{Y}}}
\end{equation}

where $k_b$ is the buoyancyForce constant and $\hat{\mathbf{\hat{Y}}}$ is the world-up vector.







\subsubsection{Damping and Stability}
To prevent harmonic oscillation (infinite bouncing), a damping force $\mathbf{F}_d$ is applied based on the vertical velocity $v_y$ at the floater's specific world-space position:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_d = -(v_y \cdot \zeta \cdot s_i) \hat{\mathbf{\hat{Y}}}
\end{equation}

where $\zeta$ represents the buoyancyDamper.







\subsubsection{Global Water Resistance}
When the ship is submerged ($s_i > 0$), global drag tensors are applied to the Rigidbody to simulate fluid viscosity:

\begin{align}
    \mathbf{F}_{drag} &= -\mathbf{v} \cdot C_d \\
    \mathbf{\tau}_{drag} &= -\mathbf{\omega} \cdot C_a
\end{align}

where $C_d$ is waterDrag and $C_a$ is waterAngularDrag.








\subsubsection{The Keel Effect (Lateral Resistance)}
Real ships have keels that allow forward movement but resist sideways "slipping." This is modeled using a slip angle $\theta$, derived from the dot product of the planar velocity $\mathbf{v}_p$ and the ship's forward vector $\hat{\mathbf{Z}}$.

    \begin{equation}
        \cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{v}_p \cdot \hat{Z}}{\|\mathbf{v}_p\|}
    \end{equation}


    The resulting sideways force is:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{keel} = -(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{\hat{X}}) \cdot \text{DragFunction}(1 - |\cos \theta|) \cdot k_{keel}
    \end{equation}


where $\mathbf{\hat{X}}$ is right vector, $k_{keel}$ is a coefficient.
\text{DragFunction} is set by user in spline form visible in \ref{fig:keel_drag_curve}.








\subsubsection{Hydrostatic Pressure Gradient and Wave-Slope Dynamics \label{sect:Wave-Slope-Dynamics}}

To simulate the "surfing" effect where a vessel reacts to the slope of a swell, we incorporate a force derived from the surface normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. Unlike static buoyancy, which only acts along the world-up axis $\hat{\mathbf{Y}}$, the pressure gradient in a moving fluid creates a lateral force component.

The "Wave-Slide" force $\mathbf{F}_s$ is modeled by projecting the gravitational vector $\mathbf{g}$ onto the tangent plane of the water surface. This represents the component of gravity that pulls the vessel "downhill" along the face of a wave:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_s = -(\mathbf{g} - (\mathbf{g} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}})\hat{\mathbf{n}}) \cdot k_{surf} \cdot s_i
\end{equation}

Where:
    $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the unit surface normal at the floater position.
    Variable $k_{surf}$ is a coefficient, and
    $s_i$ is the submergence ratio of the floater ($0 \leq s_i \leq 1$) taken from ~\eqref{equation:submergence_equation}.

\subsubsection{Center of Mass / Gravity (CG) Configuration}
Stability is determined by the relationship between the Center of Buoyancy (average of submerged floaters) and the Center of Mass. By lowering the CoM below the geometric center, a restoring torque is naturally generated by the physics engine, ensuring the ship remains upright. In a model ship used in simulation CG is set to be below hull. That makes the drone "unsinkable" witch is desired in small sea drones. On large ships CG is usualy much higher to prevent large stabilizing forces. If a massive ship had a CG as low as a small drone, the "Righting Moment" would be so powerful that the sudden, violent snapping back to center could cause structural fatigue or catastrophic failure in heavy seas.












\subsection{Propulsion Dynamics}

The propulsion system in the simulation assumes a simplified thrust model where the force $F_{prop}$ is directly proportional to the throttle input $\delta_t \in [-1, 1]$ and the defined motor power $P_m$.

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{prop} = \delta_t \cdot P_m \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}}_{prop}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{\hat{n}}_{prop}$ represents the forward unit vector of the propeller mount.


\subsubsection{The Propeller Wash Effect (Slipstream)}

In real-world ships, the rudder is often positioned directly behind the screw to take advantage of the accelerated flow.  SImulator models the "Effective Flow Velocity" $V_{eff}$ at the rudder as:

\begin{equation}
V_{eff} = v_z + \left( \delta_t \cdot \frac{P_m}{m} \cdot \eta_{wash} \right)
\end{equation}

Where: $v_z$ is the forward local velocity of the ship. $m$ is the mass of the vessel. $\eta_{wash}$ is the effect coefficient. This ensures that the vessel maintains maneuverability even at low hull speeds, provided the engine is engaged.


\subsubsection{Hydrodynamic Forces on the Rudder}
The rudder is treated as an airfoil generating two primary force components: Lift (the turning force) and Drag (the resistive force).



\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/airfoil_rudder.png}
    \caption{Rudder airfoil model.}
    \label{fig:rudder_airfoil}
\end{figure}


The lift magnitude $L$ is calculated using a variation of the fluid lift equation, where the force is proportional to the square of the velocity and the sine of the rudder angle $\alpha$.

\begin{equation}
L = V_{eff}^2 \cdot C_L \cdot \sin(\alpha) \cdot \text{sgn}(V_{eff})
\end{equation}

where: $C_L$ is  lift coefficient representing the rudder's effectiveness, $\alpha$ rudder angle in radians.

The act of turning the rudder introduces a drag force $D$ that acts against the forward motion of the ship, simulating the energy loss during a turn.

\begin{equation}
D = V_{eff}^2 \cdot C_D \cdot |\sin(\alpha)| \cdot \text{sgn}(V_{eff})
\end{equation}

where $C_D$ is the dragCoefficient.


The final force applied to the ship at the rudder's position $\mathbf{p}_{rudder}$ is the vector sum of the lift and drag:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{total} = (L \cdot \mathbf{\hat{Rr}}) + (-D \cdot \mathbf{\hat{Rf}})
\end{equation}

Where $\mathbf{\hat{Rr}}$ is the rudder’s right-facing unit vector and $\mathbf{\hat{Rf}}$ is the ship’s forward unit vector. Because this force is applied at a distance $d$ from the Center of Mass, it generates the turning moment required to rotate the ship.





\subsection{Summary of Forces and Moments}

The dynamic behavior of the drone ship is governed by the summation of all hydrodynamic, gravitational, and propulsion forces acting upon the rigid body. To ensure a coherent physics simulation, these forces are categorized into restorative, resistive, and active control components. The total force $\mathbf{F}_{total}$ and total torque $\mathbf{\tau}_{total}$ acting at the Center of Mass (CoM) can be summarized by the following Newtonian equations:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{total} = \mathbf{F}_g + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \mathbf{F}_{B,i} + \mathbf{F}_{d,i} + \mathbf{F}_{s,i} \right) + \mathbf{F}_{drag} + \mathbf{F}_{keel} + \mathbf{F}_{prop} + \mathbf{F}_{rudder}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{\tau}_{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\mathbf{r}_i \times \mathbf{F}_{net,i}) + (\mathbf{r}_{prop} \times \mathbf{F}_{prop}) + (\mathbf{r}_{rud} \times \mathbf{F}_{rudder}) + \mathbf{\tau}_{drag}
\end{equation}

Where $\mathbf{r}$ denotes the lever arm vector from the CoM to the specific point of force application.
